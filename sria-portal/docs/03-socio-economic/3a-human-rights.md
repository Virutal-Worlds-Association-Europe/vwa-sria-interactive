# 3a Human rights, safety, participation and societal values

## Scope

Virtual Worlds (VW) are no longer speculative technologies — they are emerging as powerful platforms
for interaction, work, culture, and commerce. Yet their development raises not only technical hurdles,
but also profound societal, legal, and economic challenges. These non-technical dimensions are not
peripheral: they are core to how VW will function, who they will serve, and whether they will reflect the
values Europe aims to uphold.

As socio-technical systems, VW must be designed and governed with principles like inclusiveness,
transparency, safety, sustainability, and fairness. Without deliberate attention to these aspects, there is a
risk that VW will replicate or even amplify societal inequalities, corporate dominance, and opaque
decision-making. Inclusive access, meaningful participation, robust oversight, and ethical design are not
just desirable outcomes—they are prerequisites for success, adoption, and legitimacy.

Importantly, these challenges are not only considerations to be “managed” after technological
deployment. Many raise urgent research and innovation questions. For example, how can algorithmic
transparency and user agency be embedded in immersive systems? What governance models ensure
both freedom of expression and effective moderation? How can business models remain competitive yet
equitable and sustainable? These are not solved by engineering alone; they demand interdisciplinary
inquiry and experimentation across legal, social, economic, and ethical domains.

European and international initiatives increasingly recognize this. Projects such as XR4Human and
OPENVERSE, as well as frameworks developed by UNESCO and the Council of Europe, emphasize the
need to build human-centric, rights-respecting virtual environments. Their work illustrates that values
like accountability and inclusivity can—and must—be designed into VW from the ground up.

This chapter addresses these issues across several thematic sections. Each section outlines key
societal or systemic challenges and formulates targeted Strategic Research and Innovation Objectives.
These include inclusion, participation and accessibility; fundamental rights and democracy; privacy and
agency; transparency and oversight; well-being; safety and consumer protection; economic and
environmental sustainability; and governance. Together, they provide a foundation for developing VW
that are not only technologically advanced, but also socially resilient and value driven.

## Foundations for inclusion, participation and accessibility

Virtual Worlds (VW) hold vast potential to reshape how we live, work, learn, and socialise, with forecasts
predicting an immersive economy in the hundreds of billions of euros within this decade. Realising these
opportunities, however, hinges on making VW inclusive, participatory, and accessible by design.

Inclusion ensures all social groups find a place and representation; participation means users have
agency and voice in VW communities and governance; and accessibility guarantees that people of all
abilities can create, use, and benefit from immersive technologies. These human-centric values are not
merely ethical imperatives but prerequisites for mainstream adoption and success. Diversity, equity, and
inclusion are foundational to a positive User Experience (UX) and key to widespread adoption.
Conversely, VW lacking inclusivity or permitting abusive behaviour risk alienating large segments of
users; a 2022 global survey found that 62% of people perceive a current lack of inclusivity, and 82%
believe companies should actively ensure accessibility. Therefore, VW must be built for and by a broad
spectrum of society, not just a privileged few.

European policymakers and international organisations increasingly recognise this need. The European
Commission’s 2023 "Web 4.0 and Virtual Worlds" strategy aims to ensure an open, secure, trustworthy,
fair, and inclusive digital environment reflecting European values and rights, making the future immersive
internet human centric. UNESCO considers truly inclusive and ethical VW "the challenge" of society’s
transition to immersive digital environments. Similarly, the ITU’s Focus Group on the virtual world
stresses inclusion, diversity, and accessibility as core principles, advocating for diverse cultures and
languages and upholding UN Sustainable Development Goals on equity. Economically, VW offer a rare
opportunity to design a more equitable system from the outset, avoiding the reinforcement of past tech
inequalities. The following research topics address key challenges and opportunities for inclusion,
participation, and accessibility, guiding a human-centric European approach.

### Research topics for inclusion, participation and accessibility
### 3a.1 Bridging the digital divide

A critical challenge for VW is the digital divide and the high-cost barrier, which currently risk excluding
large segments of society. Access to rich virtual environments often necessitates advanced Virtual
Reality (VR) and Augmented Reality (AR) hardware, high-speed connectivity, and significant computing
power. Addressing these socioeconomic barriers is crucial to prevent VW from becoming homogenous
platforms accessible only to a privileged few. Bridging this divide ensures broader participation and
democratises access to services and opportunities, such as immersive education, telehealth, and global
digital collaboration for remote or underserved populations.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
Currently, 38% of consumers are concerned about the affordability
of VW technologies. Furthermore, 2.9 billion people globally still lack meaningful internet connectivity,
and new digital frontiers like VW risk exacerbating existing inequalities if access is not broadened. Early
adopters of Extended Reality (XR) are disproportionately from wealthier groups and regions, leading to a
widening gap between those who can engage in 3D digital experiences and those who cannot. Real
world inequalities, including economic disparities and a lack of digital skills and literacy required to
engage with complex XR systems, risk being replicated or amplified in VW without deliberate
interventions. This gap highlights the need for proactive measures to make VW participation a widely
shared capability rather than a luxury.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Develop and deploy solutions to make VW access affordable and available to all, including
  research and development on low-cost XR hardware and network optimisations for efficient
  bandwidth use.
- Support initiatives to expand broadband infrastructure, examining subsidies or public-private
  partnerships to provide VR equipment in public spaces.
- Focus research on accessibility in low-resource settings, such as mobile-based or cloud
  rendered virtual experiences, to ensure inclusion of developing regions and prevent the
  exacerbation of existing inequalities for vulnerable groups.

### 3a.2  Inclusive design and standards
The technical design of VW significantly impacts their inclusivity. A lack of interoperability and common
standards can create siloed platforms, preventing users from seamlessly moving between different VW
or transferring their digital assets and identity across platforms. This fragmentation harms overall
inclusion by limiting network effects and segmenting communities. Designing for accessibility from the
outset, known as native accessibility, is equally vital. Without deliberate inclusive design, XR hardware
and software can inadvertently exclude people with disabilities, older adults, or others with special
needs, making it difficult for them to engage with immersive experiences. By establishing common
standards and applying universal design principles, VW can lower technical barriers and improve access
for all, fostering broader participation and empowering people with disabilities.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
Current VW proto platforms are often siloed, hindering seamless
user movement and asset portability. This fragmentation creates a significant barrier to broad inclusion
and limits the scalability of VW. Furthermore, many existing XR interfaces inherently assume certain user
capabilities, such as vision, hearing, fine motor control, and the ability to perceive 3D visuals. This poses
considerable obstacles for individuals who are blind, deaf, motion-impaired, or prone to cybersickness.
The absence of universal design principles as a foundational element in XR development means that
products are not inherently usable by everyone, regardless of ability. This gap, if unchecked, risks
entrenching digital divides, creating a two-tier system of compatible versus incompatible platforms and
experiences accessible only to the fully able-bodied.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Create guidelines, standards, and tools for inclusive XR design, spanning both technical and
  human factors research.
- Establish interoperability standards, including open protocols for avatars, assets, and identity, to
  build inclusive, open, and ethically responsible VW with privacy and security by design.
- Conduct research on universal design in XR to make interfaces usable for people with diverse
  sensory, physical, and cognitive abilities, including developing advanced assistive technologies.
- Establish concrete parameters to secure human interaction, such as an XR Accessibility WCAG
  equivalent, and design toolkits to enable industry to routinely build inclusive features.
- Develop certification processes or an "inclusivity score" for platforms to measure and
  benchmark accessibility in VW, encouraging competition on inclusivity.

### 3a.3 Diverse content and representation
Achieving true inclusivity in VW extends beyond infrastructure to encompass cultural, linguistic, and
representational aspects. Users need to see themselves reflected in virtual spaces, which is currently
challenging as many platforms cater to a narrow demographic. Addressing this ensures that VW are
welcoming and relevant to people from diverse genders, age groups, ethnic, and linguistic backgrounds.
Furthermore, VW offer a significant opportunity to celebrate multiculturalism, allowing users to
experience cultures and languages beyond their own, and to preserve and foster the vitality of various
languages, including those of smaller communities.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
A significant challenge lies in how identities and cultures are
represented in virtual spaces, as many platforms currently skew towards a narrow demographic, often
young, male, and Western. This can alienate individuals from other genders, age groups, ethnic, or
linguistic backgrounds; for example, avatar design options have historically failed to capture the diversity
of human skin tones, body types, clothing, or assistive devices. A survey indicated that 36% of U.S.
consumers consider extensive avatar customisation options crucial for inclusive VW. If platforms do not
provide broad linguistic and cultural representation, many people will not feel a sense of belonging. A
darker side is the replication and amplification of social biases and harms within immersive
environments, with mounting evidence of online harassment, discrimination, and hate speech feeling
more traumatic in VR/AR due to the brain perceiving virtual threats as real, embodied experiences.
Women, minorities, and LGBTQ+ users report intense incidents of sexual harassment and other toxic
behaviours, which discourages their participation. Without proper safeguards, VW risk mirroring or
magnifying real-world inequities, including racism, sexism, and ableism, and can even facilitate cultural erasure.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Promote research and creative innovation in linguistically and culturally diverse and
  representative content for VW.
- Fund experiments in multilingual, multimodal (spoken and signed languages), and multicultural
  virtual experiences, including indigenous language VR storytelling, virtual heritage sites, and
  diverse avatar assets.
- Develop inclusive avatar design frameworks that allow representation of different races, genders,
  body types, and abilities.
- Explore how representation affects user psychology and engagement to provide evidence that
  broad representation and customisation improve user retention.
- Conduct social science research, including digital anthropology, to study VW cultural parameters
  and inform guidelines that avoid cultural biases and respect differences.
- Build libraries of open, diverse digital assets—avatars, environments, and narratives—for
  communities and educators, ensuring European VW reflect Europe’s cultural and linguistic
  diversity.
- Integrate ethical norms and robust content moderation and safety-by-design features (e.g., quick
  reporting/blocking tools, personal safety bubbles) to combat harassment and hate speech.
- Explore how immersive experiences can enhance empathy and understanding across cultures
  and abilities, enabling users to "live experiences beyond their own."

### 3a.4 Empowerment through governance, education and skills
A crucial objective is building the skills and human capital necessary for inclusive VW. This involves
educational programmes and research into pedagogies that broaden the participation of currently under
represented groups in XR creation and leadership. Empowering a wide range of people with the skills to
create and critically engage with VW ensures these spaces evolve in line with societal needs and values,
rather than being dictated by a tech elite. This is essential for preventing VW from being dominated by
corporate interests or authoritarian norms and for ensuring its evolution is guided by the many, not just a few.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
Many people currently lack the digital skills to comfortably engage
with complex XR systems, which compounds the existing access gap. This highlights a need for
comprehensive training programmes to equip users with necessary skills such as digital literacy, spatial
awareness, and the ability to navigate complex virtual environments. Furthermore, there is a challenge in
ensuring that decision-making and rule-setting in VW are inclusive and participatory, preventing
potential domination by corporate interests or authoritarian norms. The absence of inclusive governance
frameworks raises concerns about rule of law, human rights, and the potential for new forms of
inequality among citizens if accessibility issues are not addressed. Decentralised technologies, while
offering opportunities for participatory governance, can also concentrate power if not deliberately made
equitable, representing a challenge in ensuring genuinely inclusive user empowerment.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Invest in educational programs and research into pedagogies to broaden the participation of
  currently under-represented groups in XR creation and leadership.
- Increase the number of women, minorities, and people with disabilities in XR development,
  design, art, and research through targeted training programs, scholarships, bootcamps, and
  mentorship networks.
- Enhance digital literacy for citizens at large, helping the public understand how VW work and how
  to engage safely.
- Create accessible tutorials, citizen hackathons in VW building, and public deliberation exercises
  to foster broader public engagement and co-creation.
- Explore multi-stakeholder governance models that involve a diverse array of stakeholders (users,
  experts, civil society, regulators, industry) in setting rules for VW.
- Develop new tools and approaches for content moderation in immersive 3D spaces, such as
  avatars of moderators, to uphold community standards without infringing rights.
- Conduct experiments in participatory governance, such as extending citizen engagement into
  virtual city twin environments for urban planning, to enhance democratic participation.

### Foundations for Democracy and Fundamental Rights
Democracy and fundamental rights form the foundational bedrock of the European Union (EU), a
principle that must be embedded and strengthened through the realisation of VW. These immersive
environments have the capacity to develop a new framework for engaging with digital technology, rooted
in human dignity, freedom, equality, solidarity, democracy, and the rule of law. The EU’s Charter of
Fundamental Rights (CFR) should serve as the guiding platform for the development, design, operation,
and impact of VW, having proven a robust source of guidance amidst digital-technological transformation.

### Research topics for democracy and fundamental rights
### 3a.5 Democracy
VW offer significant potential to enhance democratic participation by providing novel formats for people
to interact and collaborate, access public services, and foster new forms of community life. These
platforms enable untapped means for individuals to voice their ideas, opinions, and concerns, and for
stakeholders to immersively respond to these inputs, facilitating new forms of collaborative consultation
and interaction. At the same time, VW may create or exacerbate novel risks through the distortion of
reality. The dynamic engagement enabled by VW, e.g. via fully immersive environments or AR overlays,
will need to be closely scrutinised and moderated where risks are apparent. A democracy-first approach
to interaction within VW paves the way for new modes of participation and dynamically integrates
regulatory needs into their governance, enhancing accountability, particularly for digitally vulnerable
individuals, and boosting digital literacy.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
VW have promising capabilities, which can be improve new forms
of democratic engagement. The status of new forms of interaction and collaboration, and their role in
shaping Virtual World, needs to be better understood, and legally analysed, to concretise these
developments. Therein, unknown risks to political processes and existing forms of democratic
participation need to be adequately addressed, such as via effective content and conduct moderation
and enforcement mechanisms. There is a specific research gap concerning the integration of democratic
input to ensure accountability, especially where it is voiced by digitally vulnerable and intersectional
populations.  Further research is therefore necessary to fully understand and implement effective and
responsive democratic mechanisms that assess and balance the societal impact of new innovations within VW.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Research on democracy, including as a principle of fundamental rights, through VW via new
  forms of collaboration, interaction, accountability, and participation.
- Research and innovation on collaborative and interactive tools for the purposes of democratic
  engagement through VW.
- Research and innovation on accountability mechanisms in VW.
- Research on democratic participation for the governance of VW.
- Research on democratic expression by digitally vulnerable and intersectional populations
  through and in VW.
- Conduct research concerning tools to moderate conduct/content in VW to fight against
  distortion of reality.

### 3a.6 Fundamental Rights
The EU Charter of Fundamental Rights enumerates a vast range of rights and freedoms, which can be
profoundly affected by and uniquely realised within VW. Personal rights, including the right to physical
and mental integrity, respect for family life, home, communications, and the protection of personal data,
are particularly pertinent. Civic rights, crucial for democratic participation, can be advanced by
innovating on user-centred design principles within VW. Furthermore, these environments can enhance
and challenge freedoms of thought, expression, information, assembly, and association, allowing
participants to meet and exchange ideas in new collaborative ways. Especially considering new forms of
technological manipulation, VW can threaten and distort the formation and dissemination of ideas.
Important social and economic rights, including the right to engage in work, freedom to conduct a
business, workers’ rights to information, and intellectual property (IP) rights, can also be enabled,
fostering new economically and socially sustainable approaches in digital environments. A proactive
approach to promoting openness, inclusion, and accessibility is paramount for the EU’s unique strategy towards VW.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
While VW hold potential for enhancing fundamental rights, their
current implementations can also affect these rights, necessitating careful consideration during design
and deployment. There is a research gap in ensuring that the design and implementation of new
technologies within VW consistently align with the CFR’s provisions, particularly concerning the equality
of persons. This includes addressing issues of non-discrimination, cultural diversity, gender equality, and
the protection and accessibility for children, the elderly, and persons with disabilities. With the CFR
serving as a vital source of inspiration, further research and innovation are essential to translate these
principles into tangible, equality-centred VW, ensuring that the guiding principles on equality are not
merely possible but become a lived reality for all users.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Research and innovation concerning the frameworks for translating fundamental rights into the
  context of VW, including specific personal, civic, political, economic, and social rights.
- Research and innovation concerning the protection and guarantee of physical and mental
  integrity, respect for private and family life, home and communications in VW.
- Promoting and advancing fundamental personal freedoms, addressing risks and threats to the
  freedoms of thought, of expression and of information within and through VW.
- Investigate VW’ contribution to the guarantee of social and economic rights, including the
  freedom of assembly, respecting the needs of a socially sustainable digital environment.
- Facilitate innovative models for VW on understanding the interplay of design approaches with
  fundamental rights guaranteeing the equality of persons, including in regard of non
  discrimination, cultural diversity, gender equality, the protection of children, the elderly and
  persons with disabilities.
- Investigate mechanisms that guarantee and support the effective implementation of
  fundamental rights concerns in VW.

### 3a.7 Technical tools supporting democracy and fundamental rights
Developing forward-looking practices for integrating democracy and fundamental rights into the lifecycle
of VW, especially practices independent of existing large players, is urgently needed. Specific technical
tools can substantially support fundamental rights considerations in digitalisation, and their application
to VW is of special relevance. These tools include conformity assessment, human oversight,
transparency requirements, and the prohibition of specific technologies. Such tools are crucial for
ensuring that VW are developed responsibly, adhere to democratic principles, and uphold fundamental
rights, thereby providing accountability and a robust framework for their deployment.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
While existing specific technical tools can support democracy and
fundamental rights in digital contexts, their role and relevance require further research and innovation
specifically for their application within VW. There is a clear gap in understanding how to best adapt,
develop, and integrate these tools into the unique lifecycle and operation of immersive environments.
Additionally, novel techniques and tools are necessary to increase the participation and responsiveness
of VW to multifaceted inputs, indicating a current deficit in advanced mechanisms for robust democratic
engagement and rights protection in these new digital spaces.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Conduct research concerning specific technical tools supporting democracy and fundamental
rights, and how they can be further developed for VW.
- Develop new insights into which VW-specific tools support democracy and fundamental rights

### Foundations for identity, autonomy and agency
VW have the potential to revolutionise digital engagement by seamlessly integrating social, economic,
and cultural activities within virtual settings, ranging from durable social immersive settings to industrial
simulations. However, these platforms simultaneously pose complex questions about identity, personal
autonomy, and agency, necessitating careful consideration of how users present themselves,
authenticate, protect their persona, and maintain control over their experiences.

### Research topics for identity, autonomy and agency
### 3a.8 Enhanced personal identity management
Identity in VW is a multifaceted concept encompassing how users present themselves (avatars), how
they authenticate, and how they protect their persona and reputation. VW offer new freedom for identity
expression, allowing users to utilise fluid and multiple identities. User identification is a priority,
balancing anonymity and trust. Decentralised identity solutions and the European Digital Identity
framework are promising approaches, offering users control over their digital identities across services
through verifiable credentials and selective disclosure. This architecture empowers users to reveal only
context-appropriate identity attributes, bridging the gap between privacy and authentication in virtual
settings, and promoting self-sovereign identity over centralised models. The goal is to enable
accountability without mandating constant real-name usage, fostering trust architectures that respect
privacy. Ensuring inclusive and user-centric identity representation, particularly through diverse avatar
design options, is also crucial for allowing free and safe expression of personal characteristics.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
While VW offer freedom in identity expression, they raise critical
challenges around authenticity, accountability, and inclusion. Reputational identity, now portable across
cross-platform ecosystems, becomes vulnerable to profiling and appropriation. Traditional notions of
identity must be harmonised with the fluid, self-generated digital persons prevalent in VW, raising
societal, legal, and ethical questions regarding the preservation of digital identity, the possibility of a
single identity across different VW via technologies like blockchain, and the limits on avatar choice or the
creation of autonomous AI-powered avatars. The continuous research and innovation challenge lies in
balancing anonymity and accountability, particularly concerning identity fraud, malicious
impersonations, identity hacks (like deepfakes), and new forms of abuse, including virtual sexual assault
and image-based abuse.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Create secure, user-centric identity solutions tailored to VW, including developing decentralised
  digital identity systems and standards aligned with the European Digital Identity.
- Enable users to verify attributes without exposing their full identity, balancing anonymity and
  accountability, and include safeguards against impersonation and identity theft.
- Explore an "inclusive design approach" to ensure avatars and identity features accommodate
  various disabilities, cultures, and preferences.

### 3a.9 Safeguards for user autonomy and mental well-being
Protecting users from manipulative, harmful, or addictive experiences in VW is paramount to ensuring
their autonomy and mental well-being. Immersive technologies can empower users by offering new
forms of agency, such as the freedom to build and explore entire virtual societies. However, they also
possess the capacity to erode agency through design features that subtly manipulate behaviour. The
core problem solved is ensuring that users remain in control of their virtual experiences rather than being
controlled by the technology or platform operators. Safeguards are particularly critical for minors, who
are at increased risk from the potential negative psychological and physical health effects of immersive environments.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:** Influential system design poses a significant risk to user autonomy
and agency in VW. Gamified feedback loops, adaptive advertising, algorithmically generated
interactions, and AI-powered non-player characters (NPCs) can nudge users toward certain behaviours,
often without meaningful awareness, complicating the boundary between consent and manipulation.
The realistic simulation of sensory inputs in VR makes it a powerful medium for influence, where users
may struggle to distinguish the results of choices from nudged ones manipulated outcomes, potentially
undermining critical thinking. In this way, VW risk eroding democratic and fundamental-rights-based
values. Social pressures, such as hyper-realistic self-customisation leading to conformity, can also
affect user agency, potentially undermining diversity. Addiction and dependency are concerns, as users
may feel unable to disengage, preferring virtual life to real life, which impairs broader autonomy. Physical
and psychological health effects, like motion sickness or blurred reality boundaries, might reduce
rational decision-making. Platform power and user agency also present a governance issue, as extensive
control by VW platforms over rules, content moderation, economic terms, and data usage can
undermine users’ agency as digital citizens. Ensuring effective transparency and human oversight for
high-risk AI systems in these environments remains a challenge.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Research and implement measures to protect users from manipulative, harmful, or addictive
  experiences in VW.
- Develop detection tools for manipulative patterns and create opt-out or "safe mode" settings to
  limit persuasive content.
- Implement content moderation mechanisms capable of handling impersonation and hate appearances.
- Conduct interdisciplinary studies on the cognitive effects of immersive tech to inform legal and
  ethics guidelines, defining and prohibiting unacceptable practices.
- Ensure virtual environments enhance human agency through features like informed choice
  prompts and time reminders to prevent overuse, rather than exploiting vulnerabilities.

### 3a.10 Real-world Extended Reality usage understanding and its societal impacts
It is crucial to gain an empirical understanding of real-world XR usage and its societal impacts to inform
effective ethical, societal, economic, and legal discussions. Currently, many of these discussions are
based on anticipated scenarios rather than concrete evidence. By conducting interdisciplinary research,
this objective aims to provide the necessary data and insights to ground policy, design, and regulatory
responses in actual user experiences across diverse populations. This approach enables the
identification and mitigation of tangible problems, fostering the development of VW that genuinely serve
societal needs and values.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**The primary research gap is that current ethical, societal,
economic, and legal discussions regarding VW are largely based on anticipated scenarios rather than
empirical evidence of real-world XR usage. There is a pressing need for empirical research to answer
specific questions, such as how different populations—including children, adults, and vulnerable
groups—experience identity, privacy, autonomy, agency, and safety in current VR platforms.
Furthermore, understanding the kinds of manipulative techniques already observable in VR and the
identity-related conflicts (e.g., impersonation, hateful avatar expressions) that communities are
encountering is essential. Without this interdisciplinary empirical research, discussions remain
theoretical, limiting the ability to develop practical, evidence-based solutions for the challenges posed by immersive technologies.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Conduct more interdisciplinary work to understand real-world XR usage and its societal impacts.
- Implement empirical research to answer questions such as how different populations (children,
  adults, vulnerable groups) experience identity, privacy, autonomy, agency, and safety in current VR platforms.
- Investigate observable manipulative techniques in VR and identity-related conflicts encountered
  by communities (e.g., impersonation, hateful avatar expressions).

### 3a.11 Adaptive and future-proof regulation
Continuously studying the emerging risks of VW and feeding these findings into agile policy development
is crucial for ensuring that regulatory frameworks can keep pace with rapid technological evolution. This
objective supports ongoing horizon-scanning to anticipate new challenges, such as those posed by AI
driven virtual agents on user rights. It promotes the design of adaptive regulatory approaches that can be
updated as VW evolve, potentially through regulatory sandboxes for testing new rules. This ensures that
legal and educational responses remain effective, allowing for the analysis, evaluation, and
implementation of applicable existing and new legislation, thereby balancing regulatory compliance with
innovation facilitation, which is essential for Europe's leadership in digital transformation.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**The rapid advancement of VW inherently creates emerging risks
that outpace traditional regulatory cycles, necessitating a continuous study of these risks and agile
policy development. A significant gap exists in anticipating novel challenges, such as the implications of
AI-driven virtual agents on user rights. Current regulatory approaches may not be sufficiently adaptive or
future-proof to manage the evolving complexities of immersive technologies. Furthermore, ensuring
effective transparency and human oversight for high-risk AI systems embedded in XR platforms remains
a challenge. There is also a need for a common taxonomy and metrics for XR harms, as well as further
research on evolving rights such as "perceptual autonomy" and "cognitive autonomy," indicating a
conceptual and practical gap in fully defining and addressing the scope of rights in advancing immersive tech.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Continuously study the emerging risks of VW and feed findings into agile policy development.
- Support ongoing horizon-scanning research to anticipate new challenges, such as AI-driven
  virtual agents on user rights.
- Promote the design of adaptive regulatory approaches that can be updated as VW evolve,
  potentially via regulatory sandboxes.
- Analyse, evaluate, and implement applicable existing and new legislation, including GDPR,
  eIDAS, DMA, DSA, and the AI Act.
- Establish common taxonomy and metrics for XR harms (e.g., privacy breaches, manipulation severity).
- Conduct further research on rights like “perceptual autonomy” and “cognitive autonomy” as
  immersive tech advances.

### Foundations for Protection of Minors
The immersion enabled by VW and the anticipated seamless integration between the physical world and
the VW suggest that future generations may live in a reality where the distinction between online and
offline experiences becomes blurred — or at least, no longer important.

VW open new frontiers for children engagement in learning, play, and socialisation. In educational
environments, VW can enhance skill-based learning, particularly in foreign language acquisition;
significantly expand the range of “hands-on” learning experiences available to children; as well as
introduce functionalities and opportunities for learners to engage with knowledge in ways not previously
possible. They also offer new forms of social interaction and cultural events, and could be used in the
treatment of mental health illness.

However, VW introduce unprecedented risks that go beyond those posed by traditional ICTs. While many
of the risks children face mirror those encountered by adults, their developmental stage and heightened
vulnerability mean the potential for harm is significantly greater. To ensure they can engage with VW
safely and ethically, an interdisciplinary approach—encompassing child psychology, education, law, and
human-computer interaction—is essential. Only through such collaboration can we design virtual
spaces that prioritize children’s best interests from the outset.

### Research topics for protection of minors
### 3a.12 Privacy, data protection, and unfair commercial practices
One of the primary challenges XR technologies pose to personal data protection is the sheer volume and
diversity of data they collect as discussed above, which can lead to data breaches; biased or unjust
inferences; decisions impacting people’s lives and opportunities; as well as manipulation and
exploitation (both commercially and politically), as these technologies may ultimately understand users
better than they understand themselves. Considering that children are more open to external influence
and merit specific protection regarding their personal data, as they may be less aware of the risks,
consequences and safeguards concerned and their rights in relation to the processing of personal data
(Recital 38 GDPR), age-appropriate measures should be in place. Age-appropriate design means that the
default state of a platform actively promotes children’s best interests, as match children’s evolving
capacity. Embracing this approach may ensure children’s rights and well-being are prioritized from the
ground up, rather than considered as afterthoughts.

*Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
Despite growing recognition of the risks that VW pose to personal
data protection, there is a significant gap in aligning the processing of children’s data with the principle of
the best interests of the child. Technology design often fails to incorporate age-appropriate protections
by default, leaving children exposed to data-driven harms such as manipulation, profiling, commercial
exploitation, and discrimination. Moreover, engineers and developers frequently lack training tailored to
children’s specific needs and vulnerabilities, resulting in systems that are not built with children in mind
from the outset. There is a need for research that not only advances age-appropriate design in VW but
also explores practical mechanisms—such as Privacy-Enhancing Technologies (PETs) and specialized
training for XR developers—to ensure children’s rights and well-being are embedded into these
technologies from the outset.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Age-Appropriate PETs for VW: Develop PETs specifically tailored to children’s developmental
  stages and digital behaviours in VW. This includes tools for real-time maximum data
  minimisation, consent management, age assurance mechanisms and identity protection
  designed with child-friendly interfaces.
- Embedding the Best Interests of the Child into XR Design Frameworks: Co-create with children
  and expert design guidelines and technical standards that operationalize the best interests of the
  child principle in VW. This involves translating legal, educational and psychological standards
  into actionable design patterns for developers. A rating system could also be prototyped to
  provide labels for VW that comply with child-safe design practices. 
- Child-Centred Training Programs for XR Engineers and Developers: Design and implement
  interdisciplinary training modules that equip technology developers with knowledge of child
  development, digital rights, and ethical design practices.  
- Assess how existing regulatory frameworks that protect children’s data online, such as the GDPR,
  DSA, AVMSD and AI Act, apply to online worlds and whether additional regulation or guidance is
  needed to promote a precautionary approach. 

### 3a.13 Children’s safety
VW offer rich social interactions, but these can be exploited for grooming, bullying, and access to
inappropriate content. The immersive nature of these environments makes all forms of abuse feel more
immediate and emotionally impactful than on traditional online platforms, potentially lowering a child’s
defences much more quickly. Additionally, the use of avatars introduces new forms of behavioural
cyberbullying, such as identity manipulation or avatar-based harassment. These dynamics highlight the
need for behavioural moderation—not just content moderation—to effectively protect children in
immersive virtual spaces. This is especially important in the gaming area and can be improved by AI flagging tools.

*Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
VW demand moderation approaches that go beyond traditional
content filtering to include behavioural moderation, requiring systems that can detect and address
problematic interactions in real-time, often mediated through avatars and spatialized communication.
Furthermore, a major gap exists in the adequate implementation of age assurance mechanisms that are
effective, privacy-preserving, and grounded in children’s developmental needs.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Design virtual world experiences that are age-appropriate, with strong interpersonal privacy
  protections embedded by design and by default. These environments should prioritize children's
  developmental needs and safety, ensuring that social interactions occur within clearly defined
  and controllable boundaries. This avoids unwanted contact and peer pressure. 
- Develop advanced behavioural moderation techniques for VW that can detect and mitigate
  harmful interactions—such as grooming, bullying, and avatar-based harassment—in real-time,
  enhancing the protection of children beyond traditional content moderation. Crucially, these
  technologies should not only be privacy-preserving and based on children’s rights by design, but
  also balance and respect other fundamental rights, such as freedom of expression. An important
  discussion also lies in defining the extent of gatekeeping power that platforms should have
  regarding users’ fundamental rights online. 
- Implement effective age assurance mechanisms that prevent children from accessing
  inappropriate content in VW. Develop and deploy robust, privacy-preserving technologies and
  protocols to accurately assess the age of users in immersive environments without exposing their
  identity (zero-knowledge proof technologies) 

### 3a.14 Digital and AI literacy
Children, parents, and educators must be equipped with the knowledge, understanding, and skills to
navigate VW safely and confidently. While such efforts cannot replace the need for age-appropriate,
ethically designed platforms rooted in children's best interests, targeted digital and AI literacy programs
play a crucial complementary role. These programs can empower children to recognize and respond to
risks such as grooming, manipulation, unwanted contact, peer pressure, and algorithmic influence. They
also help establish healthy digital habits, including setting boundaries around time spent online,
managing emotional responses to virtual interactions, and maintaining a clear sense of self. For parents
and educators, these tools offer guidance on how to support children’s online experiences, foster open
communication, and advocate for safer digital ecosystems.

*Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
Immersive experiences in VW are a novelty for everyone and
human beings are certainly not fully prepared to deal with the differences they present to “normal”
platforms. They introduce new psychological, social, and cognitive dynamics that users—especially
children—are not fully equipped to navigate. Children, parents, and educators often lack the necessary
digital and AI literacy, including the critical understanding of how immersive technologies affect
attention, emotion, social interaction and decision-making. There is a pressing need for guidance and
tools that help develop these competencies, especially within educational environment.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Design and test VW safety education curricula tailored to different age groups, using interactive
  scenarios that enable children to practice recognizing and responding to online risks within VW. 
- Co-design practical resources and training for parents, caregivers, and educators to support
  safe, balanced, and age-appropriate use of VW, including safety guides, household rule-setting
  tools, and supervision strategies that respect children’s autonomy. Materials may address both
  technical controls (e.g. platform settings, safe play spaces) and socio-emotional guidance (e.g.
  managing dissociation, setting time limits). 

### Foundations for safety, security and consumer protection
The rapid evolution of the European consumer perspective has brought new policy significance,
particularly within discussions on protecting consumers' economic interests. Emerging risks, stemming
from growing information and power imbalances between digital service recipients and providers, pose
new challenges for ensuring safety and security in this evolving environment, especially for vulnerable
individuals like children. The emergence of VW and AI systems necessitates further research and
innovation to maximise their potential in setting new standards for safety and fairness.

### Research topics for safety, security and consumer protection
### 3a.15 "Safety and fairness by design" principles 
VW present an unprecedented opportunity to create inclusive and safe environments that are "safe and
fair by design." Experience with digital technologies shows that VW can profoundly impact vulnerable
individuals. Amidst growing societal awareness of risks related to behavioural profiling, attention
grabbing techniques, and interface tricks (dark patterns), consumers are expected to welcome virtual
environments that protect and care for them, rather than exploiting individual weaknesses. This offers a
significant opportunity for legally informed research and innovation to address emerging vulnerabilities,
potentially fostering a "race to the top" among service providers who prioritise consumer trust, safety, and security.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
The policy debate has seen the rise of "digital asymmetry,"
reflecting a permanent state of weakness for individuals due to knowledge imbalances, lack of control
over online choice architectures, and asymmetries in contracting power, particularly in hyper
personalised environments. VW, enabled by worldwide sharing of personal data and behavioural
tracking, pose new risks by potentially capitalising on dispositional vulnerabilities. Existing protection
paradigms based on disclosure and consent are backsliding. While "fairness by design" could reverse
the burden of proof in cases where individuals are affected by this asymmetry (e.g., in hyper-personalised
environments or where nudges exploit temporary vulnerable states of mind), comprehensive implementation strategies and frameworks for VW are still needed.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Develop and implement "safety and fairness by design" principles in VW to mitigate new digital
  vulnerabilities and address digital asymmetry. 

### 3a.16 Frameworks to analyse and regulate new forms of transactions
Recent years have seen an influx of consumer-trader interactions in which money is not exchanged or
does not fully cover the counter-performance (e.g., social media platforms where data constitutes the
counter-performance). These transactions can also address novel products and services, blurring the
line between established understandings of sale, transfer of ownership and provision of services. On the
one hand, explicit recognition of these interactions as "transactions" by the Commission Guidance to the
Unfair Commercial Practices Directive is groundbreaking, extending consumer law protection to
attention and engagement markets. On the other hand, questions remain concerning a unified approach
for transacting for novel ‘objects’ within VW. Developing comprehensive frameworks to analyse and
regulate these new forms of transactions is crucial for ensuring consumer protection and fair practices
within VW. This will clarify consumer rights and safeguard market dynamics in these evolving environments.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
While interactions where data constitutes the counter
performance have been implicitly recognised as transactions, a comprehensive theoretical underpinning
for their full implications under consumer law, particularly in the context of VW, is lacking. The
Commission Guidance, although groundbreaking, is non-binding, meaning its full implications for
consumer protection in VW require further research. Additionally, a clearer legal understanding of the
objects of these transactions is needed. Some virtual objects may provide the impression of being
protected as ‘property’, whereas others are more obvious (new types of) services. There is thus a need to
develop robust analytical and regulatory frameworks that account for the unique characteristics and
complexities of transactions within immersive online environments, ensuring that consumer rights are
clearly defined and upheld, and that fair practices are enforced across all forms of value exchange.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Develop comprehensive frameworks to analyse, evaluate and regulate new forms of transactions
  in VW, ensuring consumer protection and fair practices and preventing abusive practices. 
- Based on legal analysis, foster a consistent understanding of the objects of new transactions within VW. 
- Explore the full impact of novel transactions on consumer rights and dynamics within VW
  markets, including in regard of dependencies with adjacent markets.
- Inform consumers on the legal enforceability of new forms of transactions in VW. 

### 3a.17 Fair pricing practices
Prices are a crucial signal to consumers regarding the value of goods or services. Within VW, pricing
practices – as well as similar monetisation mechanisms such as virtual currencies – must uphold the
high level of consumer protection of the EU. Rigorous research into protecting consumers from
behavioural price personalisation is required to create a level playing field. While generally not
prohibited, price personalisation based on behavioural data to assess a consumer's "willingness to pay"
is a contentious policy topic. Aspects of such practices may also be intentionally concealed to ‘gamify’
the consumer experience within VW Addressing this is vital as such techniques, while economically
beneficial to traders, are typically detrimental to consumer welfare, carry significant discrimination risks,
and can hinder consumers' ability to learn market prices and make informed choices. 

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
Despite being permissible under the updated Consumer Rights
Directive, behavioural price personalisation raises doubts regarding its reconciliation with various
consumer protection laws. It can hinder consumers' ability to ascertain market prices, affect informed
choices, increase knowledge and power imbalances between consumers and traders, and potentially
lead to anti-competitive effects, especially when behavioural data is shared among traders. Although
few providers admit to using these techniques, their economic benefits to traders are substantial, while
the detriments to consumer welfare are notable, including penalising product dependency. As VW
present unique commercial opportunities, research is needed to clarify consumers’ rights on digital
assets and to better understand and avoid unfair pricing practices within these new environments.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Evaluate the role of pricing in the context of novel transactions within VW and critically evaluate
  potential sources of abusive discrimination.  
- Develop and implement fair pricing practices in VW to protect consumers from the risks
  associated with behavioural price personalisation. 

### 3a.18 Adaptive and future-proof regulation for consumer protection
Continuously studying the emerging risks in VW and feeding findings into agile policy development is
essential for adaptive and future-proof regulation. This objective supports ongoing horizon-scanning to
anticipate new challenges, enabling the design of regulatory approaches that can evolve with VW,
potentially through regulatory sandboxes. Such adaptive regulation ensures that relevant existing and
new legislation on consumer protection, including the Consumer Rights Directive, Digital Content
Directive, General Product Safety Regulation, Unfair Commercial Practices Directive, and the Digital
Services Act, are effectively analysed, evaluated, and implemented, thereby maintaining a high level of
consumer safety and protection in dynamic digital environments.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
The rapid evolution of VW introduces new and complex risks that
existing consumer protection legislation may not fully address, necessitating continuous research and
agile policy development. There is a need for ongoing horizon-scanning to anticipate novel challenges
that arise from these immersive environments. A key gap lies in designing regulatory approaches that can
effectively adapt as VW evolve, and in implementing mechanisms like regulatory sandboxes to test new
rules. Furthermore, ensuring the effective analysis, evaluation, and implementation of applicable
existing and new consumer protection legislation within the unique contexts of VW remains a significant challenge.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Continuously study the emerging risks of VW and feed findings into agile policy development.
- Support ongoing horizon-scanning research to anticipate new challenges. 
- Promote the design of adaptive regulatory approaches that can be updated as VW evolve,
  potentially via regulatory sandboxes to test new rules. 
- Analyse, evaluate, and implement applicable existing and new legislation on consumer
  protection, including the Consumer Rights Directive, Digital Content Directive, General Product
  Safety Regulation, Unfair Commercial Practices Directive, and the Digital Services Act. 

### 3a.19 Robust cybersecurity measures
Ensuring robust cybersecurity for the infrastructure enabling VW is an overarching concern for traders,
consumers, and various economic operators. Research is vital to closely follow and scrutinise required
cybersecurity measures and obligations under the EU’s legal cybersecurity framework, including the
Cybersecurity Act, Cyber Resilience Act, Cyber Solidarity Act, and the NIS 2 Directive. A research
informed understanding of these acts will assist in operationalising their various requirements,
particularly where VW include products with digital elements (e.g., AR applications) or utilise specific
information infrastructure. This complements ongoing research in cybersecurity innovation, enhancing
the overall security of VW.

**Problem Definition and Research Gap:**
A key problem is ensuring that the cybersecurity measures and
obligations incumbent upon actors in VW effectively comply with the EU’s legal cybersecurity
framework. There is a need for research to provide a comprehensive understanding of how the
requirements within the Cybersecurity Act, Cyber Resilience Act, Cyber Solidarity Act, and the NIS 2
Directive translate into operational implementation specifically for VW. This includes contexts where VW
incorporate products with digital elements (like AR applications) or utilise specific information
infrastructure. The gap lies in connecting these legal requirements with concrete technical
implementations, ensuring that the unique cybersecurity challenges posed by immersive environments
are adequately addressed and aligned with existing cybersecurity innovation development.

**Research and Innovation Objectives:**
- Develop and implement robust cybersecurity measures for VW, ensuring compliance with
  relevant EU Cybersecurity frameworks. 
- Promote the uptake of cybersecure products and service in VW through the formulation of
  guidelines and dissemination of information to relevant stakeholders concerning their advantages.
- Provide regulatory bodies with adequate oversight concerning the cybersecurity risks relating to
  VW and providing effective enforcement tools to operators of VW implementations.
